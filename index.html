The idea

A site that's been a problem for years could power the neighborhood.

The old Arapahoe 2-26 oil and gas well off Hughs Drive is owned by the Town of Erie and is being cleaned up now. A community solar garden is one shared array of panels on that land. You don't put anything on your roof. You subscribe, and a share of what it generates shows up as credits on your electric bill.

22 acres

Town-owned land sitting fenced and empty once the cleanup wraps up.

~500+ homes

A rough estimate of how many homes a site this size could serve. A developer would confirm.

$0 upfront

Community solar is a subscription, not a purchase. No panels or equipment at your house.

Why it's worth doing

Good for your bill, good for the land.

Credits on your bill

Subscribe and a portion of the farm's output comes back as credits on your monthly electric bill. The aim is to save you a little each month.

No roof needed

Wrong roof angle, too much shade, or you just don't want panels on your house? This is one shared array, so any neighbor on the right utility can join.

A clean second life

A former well site becomes something the whole neighborhood benefits from, instead of fenced-off ground nobody can use.

Why it's worth it

Cleaner air, and a neighborhood an HOA away already doing it.

A former oil and gas site would do more good as a solar garden than almost any other use of the land. Here's the honest math.

~3,000
metric tons of CO2 kept out of the air each year, using the EPA's avoided-emissions math for a site this size.
~650
gas cars' worth of yearly emissions, gone, every year for the roughly 20-year life of the garden.
200x
more carbon offset per acre than the same land planted as forest, by EPA and national lab figures.

It's already working just up the road

In Weld County, the same county as our area, the Uncompahgre Community Solar Garden generates power for 450 Colorado homes, the City of Westminster, and the Highlands Ranch Community Association. An HOA subscribing to a shared solar farm is exactly the model Arapahoe Ridge could follow, and the developer owns and maintains the array, so the neighborhood handles none of the upkeep.

On savings, we'll keep it straight: most households save somewhere around 5 to 10 percent on their electricity, with no upfront cost and no equipment. Income-qualified homes can save more, and you can opt out anytime. Not a windfall, but steady and risk-free to join.

Common questions

The things everyone asks first.

Does this go on my roof?

No. It's one shared array of panels on the old well site. Nothing gets installed at your home. You join by subscribing.

Will it cost me anything?

Community solar is normally a subscription with no upfront cost, and the goal is to save you a bit each month rather than charge you more. The exact terms would come later, once a developer is involved.

Is the land safe?

The site is being cleaned up under state environmental rules, and any panels would sit back from the old wellhead by the distances the Town's code requires. Safety and a finished cleanup come before anything gets built.

Is this an official Town of Erie project?

Not yet. This is a neighbor-led idea. The Town has been open to the conversation, but nothing is approved or guaranteed. This page exists to measure interest and help the idea get a fair look.

When could it actually happen?

It's early. Realistically it would be after the cleanup is finished and a developer signs on, so think in terms of a longer timeline, not this year. Adding your name now is the thing that helps it move.

What do you do with my info?

Just keep you posted on this idea and show the Town how many neighbors are interested. No spam, and it isn't shared or sold.
